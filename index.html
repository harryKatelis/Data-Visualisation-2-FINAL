Mapping the Shift in Cardiovascular Health Across Australia: 2011 & 2017

Introduction

Disclaimer: The data presented in this visualisation is solely for information purposes only. It does not aim to predict future trends. This data was the most recent data collected by the Australian Insitute of Health and Welfare (AIHW) regarding geographical variation in Cardiovascular disease, and may therefore seem outdated. The only two years of data collected were 2011 & 2017. Additionally, the trend is not indicative of what occured between these years.

Cardiovascular disease (also known as CVD) encompasses a range of conditions affecting the heart and blood vessels, including coronary heart disease, stroke, heart failure, peripheral vascular disease, and rheumatic heart disease. These conditions represent a significant burden on the Australian healthcare system and remain among the leading causes of morbidity and mortality nationwide.

To present the data collected by the AIHW, the national and state-level case numbers and proportions from 2011 and 2017 will be presented, where the geographic variation will be noted. Then, the distribution of case numbers across genders and age groups will also be displayed and analysed within each state.

Specifically, case numbers are displayed for each gender group (Male and Female), with colours in the visualisations matching those used in the charts. Age groups are divided into three main categories: 18–54, 55–74, and 75+. These classifications follow the AIHW data source (noted at the end of the visualisation).

Firstly, the overall trend of cardiovascular diseases in Australia will be compared.

Overall Australian Cardiovascular Rates in 2011 & 2017

This horizontal bar chart displays the total incidence of cardiovascular disease cases across all states in Australia for the years 2011 and 2017. In 2011, there were 1,712,489 reported cases, which decreased to 1,180,165 cases by 2017.

This represents a reduction of approximately 532,324 cases, indicating a 31% decline in total cardiovascular disease burden over the six-year period.

Geographic Distribution of CVD in Australia: 2011 & 2017

The choropleth maps above visualize cardiovascular disease rates per 1,000 people across Australian states and territories for 2011 and 2017.

In 2011, Western Australia (WA) exhibited the highest disease rate at 116 cases per 1,000 people, appearing in the darkest red out of both maps. Additionally, the lowest rate in 2011 was exhibited by South Australia (SA), with 80 cases per 1,000 people. In 2017, there was a notable overall reduction in disease intensity across most regions, with Tasmania (TAS) showing the highest rate at 77 cases per 1,000 people.

Indeed, the chloropleth maps show variation geographically in terms of the rates of cardiovascular disease in Australia. More rural states like Western Australia (WA) and Tasmania show higher rates of CVD disease, likely due to more limited access to healthcare, and a more sparse population. Northern Territory (NT) exhibited the lowest average disease rate of 59 cases per 1,000 people.

Does Population have an Effect on Disease Rate?

The bubble chart below examines the relationship between total population and cardiovascular disease cases, helping to identify if larger populations correspond to higher case numbers. Each bubble represents a state, with size (area) indicating population and position showing the relationship between population size and proportion of that population with CVD. Also, the median number of cases and mean proportion (%) are shown on the chart for each year.

Click on the dropdown below the chart to change between 2011 and 2017.

In 2011, NSW and VIC dominated in case numbers, which display a just above average proportion number. TAS, however, had the highest proportion percentage of 11.9%, showing that it had a higher rate of CVD than VIC and NSW. In 2017, NSW and VIC still led absolute case counts while TAS showed the highest CVD proportion of 7.73%, which is similar to 2011. As a result, while it can be said that population numbers are proportional to case numbers, for states like TAS, this doesn't hold.

Male & Female Cardiovascular Disease Cases in 2011 & 2017

Did the Number of Male Cases Decrease at the Same Rate as Female Cases?

The slope chart illustrates how cardiovascular disease incidence rates changed between 2011 and 2017 by Gender. Overall, Female cases fell from 0.84 million to 0.51 million, a drop of 0.33 million, while Male cases decreased from 0.87 million to 0.64 million , a reduction of 0.23 million.

Gender-Based Disease Prevalence Analysis

The stacked bar charts above compare CVD cases between Male and Female across three age groups 18–54, 55–74, and 75+ for both 2011 and 2017.

Males aged 55+ showed significantly higher case numbers than Females in both 2011 and 2017, with the 55–74 age group experiencing the highest burden of disease across both genders. Notably, for 2011, the 18–54 age group showed the highest total number of cases of 641,274, whereas for 2017, the same age group had the lowest total case numbers of 18,945.

Although the overall total number of disease cases decreased from 2011 to 2017, Male cases remain higher in older age groups.

The 18–54 age group, however, shows higher CVD diseases rates in Females rather than Males.
